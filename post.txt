title: The Kingdom
subtitle: engflash #2
image: ./archive/the-kingdom.jpg
content: Tucked away in one corner of my school was a collection of wooden classroom benches and desks, some decrepit, others broken, piled haphazardly into a heaping mass that grazed the hall ceiling. Despite the incessant cacophony of the studentry, the mass stood, silently, in its very own space-time pocket, uncontaminated by the theatrics native to the creatures its constituents once served. If they had before been mere furniture whose value laid solely in their usefulness to the commodification of excellence, they were now kings and queens of their essence, self-rulers who, though scarred and blackened, were nonetheless emancipated from a cause beyond their comprehension. The newfound freedom brought a swath of land - a self-study hall - annexed from their previous masters, upon which they built their Utopia.
It was surreal, eerie even, beholding the Kingdom by oneself; perhaps the lack of companionship rendered us more vulnerable to those whose number could afford them the luxury of congregation. Or perhaps to us humans, such an assemblage was unfathomable: how could our obsequious slaves escape the fate resigned to them since birth? Were we wrong for sitting on them, leaving unwiped coffee stains that seeped in and poisoned their flesh, defiling them with invectives when we turned recalcitrant? How come they did not exact any revenge, despite all that? The more we pondered, the more we feared its imposing presence; we dreaded being enticed by the anomaly into assimilation. In an unconscious spasm of guilt in our feeble brains, we could squeeze our way, like the entranced hikers drawn to Amigara Fall, into its aphotic center, where a grotesque metamorphosis transformed us into the Kingdom’s faithful subjects. Would our fear be palliated by then? Who could tell?
The school’s intention to dishonorably discharge all desks and benches in its service expanded the Kingdom: every now and then, one could catch a glimpse of a heavily chipped desk or three-legged chair nested against the original pile. Ignoring the sound of water from the refill station trickling into my bottle, I rid my lungs of the frigid morning air and contemplated how the pellucid sunrays, once able to light the capacious self-study hall, never quite penetrated the Kingdom’s inner walls, all the while reminding myself, like an overprotective parent to his disobedient child, not to gaze too long into the abyss before me, lest I could not resist its offer of a fatal panacea.
The mass grew like a neglected tumor, as did my doubts that some had fallen to its curse, that I could be the next saved victim. Images of the Kingdom encroached on my dreams, phantasmagorical visions of a desolated building littered with a plethora of dilapidated teaching instruments. In the middle of the schoolyard, no, the yard, for the institution then no longer belonged to us, were two rows, one desks and the other benches, precisely stacked into towers, resembling the bisected praying cosmonauts in the hellscape of Tatsuki Fujimoto. What their existence was meant to welcome, what paeans they were made to sing, one dared not to fathom.
Drenched in sweat, I prayed that the message of the Kingdom’s Horla, if mischievous, only a ruse; if a projection, never prescient.
